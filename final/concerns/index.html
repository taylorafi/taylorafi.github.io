Concerns

Safety

The joy in this new mode of transportation has caused electric scooter users to overlook some major issues that these scooters could bring about.

Electric scooters may seem fun, but they have been causing a noticeable number of accidents. The Swedish Transport Administration conducted a study in Stockholm using datasets from reported injuries to Folksam Insurance and the Swedish Traffic Accident Data Acquisition database (STRADA). As you can see, primarily 18-24 year olds are using these devices, making it a primary issue amongst college-age kids.

Dr. Joann Elmore at UCLA and her team of researchers conducted a study on e-scooter injuries after they were noticing a rise in incidents within the Los Angeles region in UCLA's outpatient clinics, emergency departments and urgent care centers.

The Study revealed shocking results:

  • Before the widespread use of e-scooters in the city, there were only about 13 injuries per year, which increased to 595 in 2018 and 672 in 2019 around the time ride-share scooter companies started to explode.
  • There have been an estimated 115 injuries per million in their health system, which surpases the national average rate of 104 injuries per million for motorcycles.

I don't know about you, but that is pretty shocking...

Health

Everyone has been in these college kids' shoes.

The majority of a college kids' day to day activities include:

  • sleeping,
  • studying and doing school work,
  • stressing out about classes,
  • eating junk food,
  • and, of course, drinking alcohol.

That does not leave much room for physical activity and their primary source of moving around becomes their walks across campus to class. However, e-scooters are actively eliminating what's left of any sort of work out for students.

In a study published by the U.S. Department of the Interior Office of Occupational Safety and Health, researchers found that individuals can burn about nine times more energy by walking to their destination than they would riding a scooter.

Yes, the scooters may save time when you're in a pinch. However, if using a scooter becomes a habitual practice to just sleep in longer before a class or because you're too lazy to walk, we may be in danger.

Do we really want to end up like the movie WALL-E where people are floating around on chairs?
